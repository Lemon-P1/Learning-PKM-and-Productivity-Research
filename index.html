Section 2
Learning Science

Modern Learning Strategies That Actually Work

2.1 Your brain‚Äôs constraints (Cognitive Load)

Your working memory is small and easily overwhelmed, while your long‚Äëterm memory is vast but slow to build. Cognitive Load Theory focuses on designing learning so your limited mental bandwidth goes into understanding, not fighting clutter and confusion.

  • Intrinsic load ‚Äì how complex the topic is by nature.
  • Extraneous load ‚Äì friction from bad design: messy slides, multitasking, noisy environments.
  • Germane load ‚Äì useful effort spent building and strengthening mental models.

A practical rule: reduce extraneous load wherever you can, and spend your limited energy on germane load ‚Äì the effort that deepens understanding.

2.2 Five high‚Äëimpact learning strategies

These strategies are well‚Äësupported by research and give you the best return on time invested.

  1. Retrieval practice ‚Äì test yourself by recalling information from memory instead of rereading. Use questions, summaries, and problem‚Äësolving to bring ideas back to mind.
  2. Spaced practice ‚Äì revisit material across days and weeks rather than cramming once. This strengthens long‚Äëterm retention.
  3. Interleaving ‚Äì mix related topics or problem types instead of practicing the same thing in long blocks. This trains you to pick the right method in varied situations.
  4. Elaboration & examples ‚Äì explain ideas in your own words and tie them to concrete examples from your life or work.
  5. Dual coding & signaling ‚Äì combine words with simple visuals and use clear structure (headings, arrows, highlights) to make material easier to follow.
Memory shortcut: Test yourself, spread sessions out, mix topics, explain ideas, and sketch or highlight structure. That combination beats almost any amount of rereading.
Section 3
Study Workflow

Designing a Learning Session: Before, During, After

3.1 Before: prepare your brain

  • Define outcomes: ‚ÄúAfter this, I should be able to explain X, solve Y, or apply Z.‚Äù
  • Activate prior knowledge: quickly list what you already know or suspect about the topic.
  • Clean the environment: one app or window, notifications off, book note template open and ready.

3.2 During: active instead of passive

  • Preview: skim headings, bold terms, and figures; predict what is coming.
  • Chunk and pause: read a small section, then close the source and recall key points in your own words.
  • Ask questions: write 1‚Äì3 questions the section answers instead of copying sentences.
  • Mark for later: highlight only what you expect to reuse, and annotate why it matters.

3.3 After: schedule remembering

  • Within 24 hours, write a short free‚Äërecall summary and compare it to the source.
  • Convert raw notes into structured notes in your PKM (literature and permanent notes).
  • Add the most important ideas to your spaced repetition system to review over time.
Micro‚Äëhabit: Always end a session by writing what changed in your understanding. If nothing changed, the session was probably too passive.

Book Note‚ÄëTaking and Remembering System

Section 4
PKM

Personal Knowledge Management: Building a Second Brain

PKM is how you store and connect what you learn so it stays findable and usable long after the original source fades.

4.1 PARA: organizing by action

PARA groups everything in your digital life into four buckets so you always know where to put and find information.

  • Projects ‚Äì short‚Äëterm efforts with clear outcomes.
  • Areas ‚Äì ongoing responsibilities that must be maintained.
  • Resources ‚Äì topics and interests you might draw on later.
  • Archives ‚Äì inactive items you may reference in the future.

The strength of PARA is execution: it keeps information aligned with what you are actively doing rather than buried under abstract categories.

4.2 Zettelkasten‚Äëstyle notes: organizing by ideas

Zettelkasten emphasizes many small, self‚Äëcontained notes linked by meaning to create a web of ideas.

  • Fleeting notes ‚Äì quick, temporary captures of thoughts and references.
  • Literature notes ‚Äì distilled notes from specific sources, written in your own words.
  • Permanent notes ‚Äì one idea per note, clearly explained and connected to related notes.

Over time, this network turns your notes into a thinking partner that surfaces patterns and supports original ideas.

4.3 A practical hybrid

A simple hybrid approach is to use PARA at the folder or database level and Zettelkasten principles for the individual notes within.

  • Maintain top‚Äëlevel buckets for Projects, Areas, Resources, and Archives.
  • Inside them, write short, focused notes with clear titles and links.
  • Keep a capture inbox and process it into literature and permanent notes daily or every few days.
Rule of thumb: Every important new idea should live in its own note and link to at least one other note so it never becomes an orphan.
Section 6
Mental Productivity

Designing for Mental Productivity and Focus

Productivity is less about squeezing in more tasks and more about designing your environment and schedule so your limited attention can go deep when it matters.

6.1 Cognitive‚Äëload‚Äëaware work

  • Minimize extraneous load: fewer tools, fewer inboxes, minimal notifications, clear file and note naming.
  • Manage intrinsic load: break complex work into smaller steps and sequence from simple to complex.
  • Invest in germane load: reflect after tasks, write short ‚Äúwhat I learned‚Äù notes, and update relevant permanent notes.

6.2 Deep work blocks

  • Schedule 45‚Äì90 minute blocks for demanding tasks with one clear objective.
  • Use one app or window, no notifications, and a visible checklist for the current block.
  • For learning, focus these blocks on retrieval, problem‚Äësolving, and note creation rather than passive consumption.

6.3 Weekly integration

A short weekly review keeps your system aligned with your real life instead of drifting into a hobby.

  • Review active projects and ask what skills or information each one needs next.
  • Check key life areas (health, career, finances) and capture insights as notes.
  • Clean your PKM inbox, merge or split messy notes, and link new ideas to existing ones.
